Research

Project I: Giant Eruptions in Massive Stars and their Effect on the Stellar Structure:
Giant eruptions (GEs) in Luminous Blue Variables (LBVs) are prolonged mass-loss events that significantly alter the star’s properties. Using MESA, we model a 70 solar mass star undergoing a GE, where initial expansion (3 days) is followed by years of contraction, releasing gravitational energy and reducing luminosity by an order of magnitude. After 87 years, the star regains equilibrium, potentially triggering another GE or a cycle of eruptions.

Project II: Accretion and Recovery in the GEs of massive stars:
In a binary system, a Giant Eruption (GE) allows the companion star to accrete mass with the high accretion rates. Using numerical simulations, we analyze how accretion affects the companion’s structure and evolution. Our grid explores 20–60 solar mass stars with accretion rates of 0.0001–0.1 solar mass per year over 20 years. For rates below 0.01 solar mass per year, the star remains hot with a slight luminosity increase. Above this threshold, it undergoes rapid inflation, cooling, and a luminosity rise of about an order of magnitude.

Project III: High Power Accretion in Massive Binary Systems and Impact of Metallicity
During a Giant Eruption in a binary system, the companion star can accrete significant mass, altering its properties and evolution. We simulate 20 and 30 solar mass stars at metallicities Z=0.02, 0.008, 0.004, with accretion rates from 0.00001-0.01 solar mass per year. At low rates, the star remains hot, while at higher rates, it inflates and cools, with this transition varying by mass. We find that higher metallicity stars show greater variations in accretion luminosity, contrary to expectations that Galactic metallicity would yield smaller variations than in the LMC and SMC. This suggests a complex metallicity dependence of accretion luminosity.

Project IV: Effect of gravity darkening and oblate factor in rapidly rotating massive stars
With collaboration: Prof. Michel Cure, and Prof. Ignacio Araya
Our study investigates the transition region where the fast solution of radiation-driven winds in a massive rotating star (velocity higher than 75% of the critical velocity) switches to the Omega-slow solution. We are focusing on the co-existence interval where both solutions simultaneously exist and the transition point where fast solutions switch to Omega-slow solutions. , in the presence of Gravity Darkening and distortion of the star’s shape due to high rotational speed.

Project V: Modeling Multiple Giant Eruptions and Recovery Phases in Massive Stars:
Some massive stars undergo major mass loss events before core-collapse supernovae. We simulate a 100 solar mass star experiencing a cycle of three Giant Eruptions (GEs) with mass loss rates of 0.01 and 0.1 solar mass per year, each lasting one year. We track the recovery phase, finding that recovery times vary with eruption history and metallicity. Higher mass loss rates lead to oscillations and compression in the outer layers, especially at higher metallicity.

Schools

MESA Summer School 2023
I attended the MESA Summer School on the stellar evolution code MESA, where I gained in-depth knowledge of the code and its applications. During the program, I had the opportunity to interact with the developers of MESA and explore fundamental astrophysical concepts through numerical simulations. The course covered a wide range of topics, including the structure and evolution of red giants, asteroseismology of early-type stars, Cepheids and nuclear reactions, stellar internal dynamics, and the evolution of massive binary systems

Stellar Winds in Extreme Massive Stars
I attended the POEMS workshop in 2023 in the Czech Republic, where we focused on stellar winds and the analysis of observational data to generate spectra. During the workshop, I gained hands-on experience with spectral analysis techniques and learned to use several advanced codes, including CMFGEN, HYDWIND, APPEAL, and SynCpec, to study the properties of massive stars through spectroscopy.
