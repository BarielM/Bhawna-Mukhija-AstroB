Research

I study the evolution of very massive stars, with a focus on their behavior near the Eddington limit, where radiation pressure rivals gravity. In this regime, stars become unstable and may undergo giant eruptionsâ€”violent mass-loss episodes that can precede supernovae. I investigate the instabilities that trigger these eruptions, as well as the hydrodynamic recovery phase that follows, using numerical simulations to understand how the stellar structure reorganizes after such events. My research also explores the role of binary interaction in shaping massive star evolution. Many observed eruptions may be driven by accretion events or mergers with a companion. I model these interactions and apply eruption physics to other massive stars, including yellow hypergiants, which experience strong mass loss in their post-red-supergiant phase. I also use the CMFGEN code to model synthetic spectra during accretion phases, linking dynamic processes to observable features. In addition, I study rapidly rotating Be stars, known for their equatorial outflows and circumstellar disks. Using the HYDWND hydrodynamic code, I investigate their wind dynamics and characterize their rotational behavior. My broader goal is to understand the feedback between radiation, mass loss, and accretion in massive stellar systems through a combination of theoretical modeling, numerical tools, and observational diagnostics.
